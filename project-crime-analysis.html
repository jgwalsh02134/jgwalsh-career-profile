Advanced Analysis of Albany Crime Data (2021-2024)

An interactive dashboard exploring crime trends, compositions, and system flows through a series of advanced data visualizations.

Visualization 1: Interactive Time-Series of Crimes and Arrests

Objective: To provide a granular, month-over-month visualization of total reported crimes versus total arrests, allowing stakeholders to identify seasonal trends, anomalies, and the persistent gap between criminal incidents and law enforcement apprehensions.

Methodological Rationale: A simple annual line chart, as seen in the source report, obscures significant intra-year fluctuations. An interactive monthly time-series offers superior analytical depth, enabling the identification of seasonal patterns (e.g., potential summer increases in certain crimes) that are critical for dynamic resource allocation and predictive modeling. The dual-axis presentation starkly visualizes the crime-to-arrest gap, a key performance indicator for law enforcement effectiveness. This approach aligns with best practices in temporal data analysis.

  • Hover-over Data: Users can mouse over any point on the lines to see the precise number of crimes or arrests for that month.
  • Toggle Traces: Users can click on the legend to hide or show the "Total Crimes" or "Total Arrests" lines, allowing for focused analysis.
  • Zoom and Pan: The chart is fully zoomable, enabling detailed examination of specific periods.

Visualization 2: Interactive Sunburst Chart of Violent Crime Composition

Objective: To provide an interactive, hierarchical view of the composition of Part I Violent Crimes across the four-year period, highlighting trends like the spike in rape cases in 2024.

Methodological Rationale: The static stacked bar chart in the source report makes it difficult to compare proportions year-over-year. A sunburst chart is a modern visualization technique superior for representing hierarchical data. It clearly shows part-to-whole relationships across multiple levels (Year -> Crime Type), facilitating a more intuitive understanding of compositional shifts. This aligns with the NIST Cybersecurity Framework's "Identify" function by helping to better characterize the nature of the "threat" landscape.

  • Hierarchical Drill-Down: Users can click on a year in the center to "zoom" into that year's specific breakdown of violent crimes.
  • Hover-over Insights: Mousing over any segment reveals the crime type, the year, the number of incidents, and its percentage of the total for that level.
  • Root Navigation: Users can click the center of the sunburst to return to the top-level view.

Visualization 3: Interactive Treemap of Crime Distribution by Neighborhood

Objective: To visualize the concentration of crime across Albany's neighborhoods in a way that allows for easy comparison of volume. This replaces the standard bar chart with a more engaging and spatially efficient format, helping to pinpoint areas for targeted interventions under the "Economic Diversification" pillar.

Methodological Rationale: A bar chart is effective for comparing values, but a treemap excels at showing part-to-whole relationships and relative magnitudes within a constrained space. It immediately draws the eye to the largest contributors (West Hill, Pine Hills). Critically, this format also effectively visualizes the scale of the "Unknown/Not in NEU Zone" data category, highlighting a significant data limitation that must be addressed for precise geographic analysis. The interactivity adds layers of data (yearly breakdowns) not possible in a static chart.

  • Proportional Sizing: Each neighborhood is represented by a rectangle, with its area directly proportional to its total crime count from 2021-2024.
  • Hover-over Details: Mousing over a neighborhood reveals its total crime count and the breakdown of crimes for each year (2021-2024).
  • Color-Coding: Rectangles are color-coded to provide an additional layer of information, such as the year with the highest number of reported crimes for that neighborhood.

Visualization 4: Sankey Diagram of Case Resolution Flow (2024)

Objective: To illustrate the flow of criminal cases through the justice system for the year 2024, from the total number of reported crimes to arrests and final case dispositions (open vs. closed). This provides a powerful, intuitive representation of the case attrition rate, directly addressing the report's call for improved transparency and oversight and supporting the "Civic Accountability & Transparency" pillar.

Methodological Rationale: Quantifying case resolution simply as a percentage (e.g., "45% of cases remained open") lacks impact. A Sankey diagram is an advanced visualization specifically designed to depict flows and their magnitudes between nodes. It makes the "funnel" effect of the justice system immediately apparent, showing how a large number of initial crimes narrows down to a smaller number of arrests and closed cases. This provides a clear, data-driven narrative about systemic efficiency and potential bottlenecks.

  • Flow Highlighting: Mousing over a flow (link) or a category (node) highlights the entire pathway, showing the magnitude of cases moving from one stage to the next.
  • Detailed Tooltips: Hovering provides the exact number of cases in each flow.
